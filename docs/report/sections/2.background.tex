\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{cfDNA fragmentomics}
cfDNA fragments are not random shears: they are produced by a programmed combination of apoptotic caspase-activated DNase, DNase1L3, and other endonucleases acting on chromatinized DNA, and the resulting fragment-length distribution carries a $\sim$10.4-bp periodicity reflecting nucleosome rotational positioning \citep{snyder2016,jiang2020}. We use input features drawn directly from this literature. Fragment length carries the dominant signal, with the canonical $167$~bp mononucleosomal mode and secondary peaks at $\sim 332$~bp di-nucleosomal and $\sim 500$~bp tri-nucleosomal multiples \citep{snyder2016}. \citet{sun2019orientationaware} showed that orientation-aware fragmentation patterns near transcription start sites correlate with tissue of origin, motivating our orientation feature. \citet{zhou2022} characterized end-motif distributions that distinguish cancer from healthy plasma, motivating our 4-mer end-motif feature at both 5' and 3' ends. \citet{cristiano2019delfi} demonstrated that genome-wide fragmentation profiles (DELFI) predict cancer status, providing the methodological precedent for fragment-feature-based prediction. FinaleMe \citep{liu2024finaleme} unified these signals into a sequence-level HMM that predicts methylation. HAVI-Methyl takes the same input feature set—fragment length, end motif, sequence context, coverage, orientation, and inferred nucleosome distance—but treats them as observations in a hierarchical latent-variable model rather than emissions of a per-sample HMM.

\subsection{Variational Bayesian inference}
Given observations $X$ and latents $Z$, variational inference \citep{jordan1999,wainwright2008} approximates the posterior $p(Z\mid X)$ by a variational distribution $q_\phi(Z)$ chosen to maximize the evidence lower bound
\begin{equation}
\elbo(\phi) = \E_{q_\phi(Z)}[\log p(X,Z) - \log q_\phi(Z)] = \log p(X) - \KL(q_\phi(Z)\,\|\,p(Z\mid X)).
\label{eq:elbo-canonical}
\end{equation}
We organize the relevant prior work into three threads.

\paragraph{Classical and amortized VI.} \citet{jordan1999,wainwright2008,blei2017vi} laid the foundations of mean-field VI for graphical models. \citet{kingma2014vae} and \citet{rezende2014} introduced amortized VI through a recognition network $q_\phi(Z\mid X)$ trained by reparameterization gradients, which is the basis for the Set-Transformer-conditioned encoder used here.

\paragraph{Scalable VI.} \citet{hoffman2013svi} showed that for conditionally conjugate exponential-family models, natural-gradient ascent in $\elbo$ becomes a stochastic forward-pass update on natural parameters, enabling stochastic VI to scale to billions of observations. \citet{hoffmanblei2015ssvi} extended SVI to structured (non-mean-field) variational families. \citet{titsias2014doublystoch} introduced doubly stochastic VI for non-conjugate models, and \citet{kucukelbir2017advi} introduced ADVI as a general-purpose automatic VI engine. We use the population-layer SVI of \citet{hoffman2013svi}, with the non-conjugate generalization of \citet{khan2017cvi,lin2019natgrad} required because our local layer is non-conjugate (Section~\ref{sec:elbo}).

\paragraph{Expressive VI.} \citet{rezende2015flows}, \citet{kingma2016iaf}, and \citet{durkan2019nsf} replaced Gaussian variational families with normalizing flows—invertible neural maps with tractable Jacobian—yielding flexible, possibly multi-modal posteriors. \citet{burda2016iwae} introduced importance-weighted bounds (IWAE) that tighten ELBO at the cost of higher gradient variance; \citet{rainforth2018iwaeSNR} showed that the encoder gradient signal-to-noise ratio paradoxically degrades as the IWAE sample count $K$ grows, and \citet{tucker2019dreg} introduced the doubly-reparameterized gradient estimator (DReG) that fixes this problem. \citet{alemi2017vib} developed the variational information bottleneck, which penalizes $I(Z;X_{\mathrm{nuisance}})$ and provides a principled de-confounding mechanism. We combine all five threads.

\subsection{Methylation foundation models, clocks, and probabilistic methylation prediction}
Recent work has begun to train transformer-style foundation models on the methylome—CpGPT \citep{cpgpt2024} and MethylGPT \citep{methylgpt2024}—which produce powerful CpG embeddings but require methylation arrays as input. HAVI-Methyl complements these by producing methylation predictions \emph{from raw WGS}, after which any clock—PhenoAge, GrimAge, DunedinPACE, or PC-clocks \citep{horvath2013,levine2018phenoage,lu2019grimage,belsky2022dunedinpace,higginschen2022pcclocks}—can be applied. \citet{ying2024causality} recently used Mendelian randomization to identify causal CpGs for aging, motivating our use of mQTLs as instrumental anchors for identifiability. The closest precedents to HAVI-Methyl in the probabilistic methylation prediction line are DeepCpG \citep{angermueller2017deepcpg}, which uses a CNN/RNN to predict single-cell methylation from sequence and neighboring CpGs but produces point estimates without principled uncertainty; METHimpute \citep{taudt2018methimpute}, which uses an HMM to impute missing methylation but operates on bisulfite data rather than fragmentomics; and Methyl-BERT \citep{lee2018methylbert}, which uses a transformer to classify methylation read-by-read. None of these methods (i) consume raw cfDNA WGS, (ii) produce calibrated posterior intervals, (iii) pool statistical strength hierarchically across samples, or (iv) jointly infer tissue-of-origin. HAVI-Methyl provides all four.
