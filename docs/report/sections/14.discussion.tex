\section{Discussion and Limitations}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Computational cost}
HAVI-Methyl is roughly $3$--$5\times$ more expensive than FinaleMe per training run because of the encoder forward through the Set Transformer and the flow forward through the neural-spline blocks. In return, inference is amortized: a new sample requires one Set Transformer forward pass per locus rather than a full Baum-Welch fit. At biobank scale ($\sim 10\,\text{k}$ samples), the amortization advantage compounds: per-sample inference cost grows as $O(L)$ for HAVI-Methyl versus $O(L\cdot T_{\mathrm{BW}})$ for FinaleMe, where $T_{\mathrm{BW}}\sim 50$ is the typical Baum-Welch iteration count. The wafer-scale accelerator wafer-streaming recipe of Section~\ref{sec:benchmark} keeps the encoder, flow, and head as one MIMD program and avoids the latency cost of repeatedly loading and unloading FinaleMe-style per-sample HMM parameters.

\subsection{Failure modes}
Several regimes warrant caution. Deeply hypo-methylated partially-methylated domains (PMDs) saturate the end-motif signal because the locus-level $\beta$ is approximately uniform, and identifiability erodes as a consequence; the model's posterior credible intervals widen appropriately, but the point estimate remains unreliable. We propose to flag PMDs upfront from a public PMD track and downweight their contribution to downstream tissue-of-origin and clock predictions. Centromeric and pericentromeric repeats suffer from collapsed mappability, which is detectable from the per-locus mappability score; we filter these regions from the prediction set as a preprocessing step. Short reads (sub-$50$~bp) carry degraded end-motif information because the 4-mer at the cut site overlaps with the read primer; we exclude these reads at the QC stage. Low-coverage tail loci with $n^{\mathrm{frag}}<2$ are reported as posterior-prior fallbacks rather than informative posteriors, with the posterior effective sample size diagnostic from Section~\ref{sec:algo} indicating where the data are insufficient.

\subsection{Extensions}
Several extensions are natural follow-ups. Nanopore direct-methylation likelihoods, which call methylation per CpG read-by-read from raw current signal, can replace the Beta-Binomial pseudo-likelihood with a per-CpG Bernoulli (the primary form already supported by the model) and yield a tighter likelihood. Joint inference with ichorCNA \citep{adalsteinsson2017ichor} provides CNV-corrected $\beta$ values that account for tumor copy-number variation, addressing a known confounder in cancer cfDNA. Attaching a methylation foundation model (CpGPT or MethylGPT) as a downstream auxiliary head consumes the HAVI-Methyl posterior $\beta$ and produces age, clock, or disease-status predictions; this composes the two pipelines as discussed in Section~\ref{sec:intro}.

\subsection{Clinical translation}
Clinical translation of an ML-based methylation predictor faces several non-statistical concerns. The U.S. FDA's Software-as-a-Medical-Device (SaMD) framework requires that any prediction informing clinical decisions be calibrated and that calibration be auditable on an ongoing basis; the conformal coverage guarantee of Proposition~\ref{prop:conformal-restated} provides exactly this property and is a key reason the conformal wrapper is non-optional for deployed use. Subgroup performance is increasingly required for regulatory submission: the model must perform comparably across age, sex, and ancestry strata, with disparities reported and bounded. The mQTL anchor loss provides one defense, since the GoDMC catalogue is well-powered across European ancestry but undersampled in non-European cohorts; we recommend stratified evaluation and, where appropriate, ancestry-specific anchor sets. Finally, prospective validation on longitudinal cohorts (with repeat plasma sampling at $\ge 6$-month intervals) is the standard requirement for deployment in MRD and early detection contexts; the ICC(2,1) reliability metric in Section~\ref{sec:benchmark} is the standard reporting choice.
