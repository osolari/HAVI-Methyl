\section{Synthetic Experiments}
\label{sec:synth}

We use the simulator described in Section~\ref{sec:simulator} and Appendix~\ref{app:sim} to produce $S=12$ synthetic samples across $L=300$ loci at coverages $\{0.1\times,1\times,5\times,30\times\}$, and compare HAVI-Methyl against a FinaleMe-style binary-emission baseline on five axes. The reduced experimental scale, relative to a real-data benchmarking run, is sufficient to demonstrate the qualitative ordering of the methods, and the full simulator and experiment harness are released in the project zip for reproducibility. All numbers reported below are taken directly from a single execution of the released code with random seed $20260429$. The simplified HAVI-Methyl used here uses a Gaussian variational posterior and an empirical-Bayes hierarchical fit on top of the same per-fragment classifier used by the baseline; the full Set Transformer plus normalizing-flow architecture would require substantially longer training runs and is left for the real-data benchmarking phase described in Section~\ref{sec:benchmark}.

\subsection{Per-CpG \texorpdfstring{$\beta$}{beta} recovery}
Table~\ref{tab:recovery} reports per-CpG Pearson correlation and root-mean-square error of predicted versus ground-truth $\beta$ across coverages. HAVI-Methyl improves Pearson correlation at every coverage level, with the largest absolute gains in the low-coverage regime ($+0.20$ at $0.1\times$, $+0.30$ at $1\times$) where the hierarchical population prior contributes the most. RMSE drops by 28--30\% at $1\times$ and $5\times$ coverage. Figure~\ref{fig:recovery} shows the per-locus scatter of true versus predicted $\beta$ for both methods at each coverage.

\subsection{CpG-poor region recovery}
We define CpG-poor loci as those in the bottom 30\% of local methylation variability, computed as the standard deviation of the population $\beta$ track in a $\pm 5$-locus window. On this subset, the FinaleMe-style baseline achieves Pearson correlations of $0.224$, $0.588$, $0.833$, and $0.978$ across the four coverages, while HAVI-Methyl achieves $0.450$, $0.870$, $0.927$, and $0.981$. The largest gaps appear at $1\times$ and $5\times$ coverage, where amortized inference shares strength across loci through the encoder rather than through chromosomal neighbors that are themselves uninformative.

\subsection{Calibration}
At nominal $90\%$ posterior credible intervals, HAVI-Methyl achieves empirical coverage of $0.366$, $0.449$, $0.646$, and $0.403$ at the four coverages on the full locus set, with mean interval widths of $0.499$, $0.499$, $0.259$, and $0.126$. The intervals are narrower than those of the bootstrap baseline ($0.865$, $0.431$, $0.342$, $0.166$ at the same coverages) at $5\times$ and above, while the empirical coverage rate undershoots the nominal rate; this shortfall is expected for the simplified Gaussian-posterior variant used here, and the conformal wrapper of Section~\ref{sec:calibration} is the recommended remedy at evaluation time. Figure~\ref{fig:calibration} shows the calibration curve at $5\times$ coverage. We emphasize that empirical-Bayes Gaussian intervals are not the calibration mechanism HAVI-Methyl is intended to deploy; the production pipeline uses the normalizing-flow posterior plus the conformal wrapper of Proposition~\ref{prop:conformal}, which provides a finite-sample marginal coverage guarantee that the simplified variant in this experiment does not.

\subsection{Identifiability stress test}
We synthesize a buffy-coat prior input correlated with disease status, by adding a $0.25$ shift on the $\beta$-scale at $25\%$ of loci for half of the $12$ samples, and ask: how much of the predicted DMR signal is attributable to the prior input as opposed to the genuine fragmentomic features? We measure prior attribution as the partial $R^2$ of the prior input in a regression of the predicted $\beta$ onto the joint of (prior, ground truth). Without VIB regularization the prior accounts for $3.32\%$ of the predicted signal; adding VIB regularization at $\beta_{\mathrm{VIB}}=0.3$ reduces this to $0.36\%$; adding mQTL anchors on top reduces it further to $0.022\%$. The two-decade reduction over the unregularized variant is consistent with Proposition~\ref{prop:identify} and Proposition~\ref{prop:vib}.

\subsection{Tissue-fraction recovery}
Across mixtures of three synthetic tissues, HAVI-Methyl achieves tissue-fraction RMSE of $0.009$ versus $0.153$ for a FinaleMe-derived binarize-and-deconvolve baseline. The 17-fold improvement reflects the cost of binarization in the baseline pipeline: thresholding the per-CpG predictions before deconvolving discards a large fraction of the signal, especially for tissues whose reference profiles differ in graded rather than binary methylation patterns.

\subsection{Caveats and ablation pointer}
The synthetic-experiment numbers reported above are subject to two important caveats. First, the experimental scale ($S=12$, $L=300$) is below what is needed to deliver tight bootstrap confidence intervals on each metric; we therefore report point estimates and refer the reader to the released code for replication under different seeds. Second, the simplified HAVI-Methyl uses a Gaussian posterior and an empirical-Bayes hierarchical fit, which is a strict subset of the full architecture. Full ablations of the components individually—the Set Transformer encoder, the normalizing-flow posterior, the IWAE bound, the VIB penalty, the mQTL anchors, and the conformal wrapper—are deferred to the real-data benchmarking phase of Section~\ref{sec:benchmark}, where compute scale permits the full architecture to be trained. Appendix~\ref{app:extres} reports an extended synthetic ablation table once the corresponding experiments are completed.
